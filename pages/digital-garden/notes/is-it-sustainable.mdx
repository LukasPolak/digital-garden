# Is It Sustainable?

Whenever you're about to change your life, one of the first questions you should ask yourself is: do I want to do this for the rest of my existence? In other words, is it sustainable?

Challenges with limited time can be exciting, but I rarely embark on a challenge that I don't want to turn into a long-term habit.

Losing weight for the beach, a billion push-ups a day, a 7-day carrot detox, a 5-day fast, and similar challenges may grab attention, make good headlines, and generate interest, but ultimately their value is shallow.

Think about it, how many people do you know who have lost weight through some crazy diet and managed to stay in shape? How many people started fasting every other day and stuck to it? Or going ice swimming every day in winter? Not many, right?

Gradually building habits for life is much more productive than forcing yourself through a challenge.
Start small and gradually increase. Find a level that doesn't exhaust you, is just slightly outside your comfort zone, and you can maintain it daily. When it becomes comfortable and ceases to be a challenge, move on to the next level.

For example, if you want to start ice swimming in January, there are better ideas than going straight to the ice lake. Most likely, you will get sick, and it will all end there. You will realize that it is not for you.

Instead, I recommend starting with something like spending a few minutes outside in just a shirt (in winter), or ending your shower with cold (or at least lukewarm) water.

When it's already normal for you and you don't have to (too much) force yourself, raise the bar higher.

For the next season, you can go to the lake when you have already gone through hundreds of cold showers and minutes of temperature discomfort. Your body will be prepared, and you will increase your chances of lasting.

## Computer Science Corelation

In computer science, it is often more productive to gradually build up a system or program rather than trying to tackle a large project all at once. By starting small and incrementally increasing the complexity, a programmer can avoid getting overwhelmed and ensure that the result is sustainable. Additionally, working on a project at a comfortable pace can prevent burnout and maintain motivation and enthusiasm for the task. Similarly, when building a habit or trying to change one's life, starting small and gradually increasing the difficulty can lead to lasting success.
